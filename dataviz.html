Who Makes the Best Board Games?

An Analysis of Board Game Designers and Publishers


Data and Goals

    Dataset Selection:
    • Data: Board Game data scraped from BoardGameGeek (BGG)
    • Data Source: Kaggle dataset by Bojan Drango
    • Data Format: CSV
    • Data Dimensions:
      • Game ID (Unique identifier for the game on BoardGameGeek)
      • Game Name (Name of the Board Game)
      • Game Type (Board Game, Video Game, Role-Playing Game, etc.)
      • Game Rating (Scale of 1-10.)
      • Game Weight (Complexity/difficulty of understanding the rules and playing the game. Scale of 1-5.)
      • Designer (Designer(s) of the game)
      • Publisher (Company that published the game)
      • Minimum Number of Players (Fewest players needed to play the game)
      • Maximum Number of Players (Most players that can play the game together)
      • Minimum Play Time (Minimum amount of time required to play the game)
      • Maximum Play Time (Maximum time needed to play the game)
      • Minimum Age (Lowest age suggested for playing the game)
      • Number of Owners (Number of BoardGameGeek community members that own this game)

    Goals:
      Many websites that list the Best Board Games or Board Game Designers based on a wide variety of subjective criteria. Do the ratings on BoardGameGeek give the same general results? Here is what I planned to do:

    • Rank Board Game Designers:
      • Create a plot of Designers sorted by the Number of Games Designed and the Average Game Rating.
    • Rank Board Game Publishers:
      • Create a plot of Publishers sorted by the Number of Games Published and the Average Game Rating.
    • Determine Best Designer/Publisher Partnerships:
      • Combination of the two previous datasets and create a plot to show the highest ranted Designer/Publisher pairings.


Elements and Evaluation


    The BoardGameGeek data set included more than just board game data. The site also consists of information and ratings for video games, role-playing games, and board game expansions. So, the first step was to filter down to just board games.
    At some point in creating the initial plots, I realized there were a lot of outliers skewing the data. Here are some issues and the solutions I applied to tidy the data:

    • Some games had a perfect 10 rating, generally due to the game having just a few ratings. In one case, a game designer had 10 games to their credit, which they themselves had rated a 10. That was the only rating each of those games had. How do I avoid having those situations skew the data? Well, for a designer or publisher to be considered "successful," shouldn't a lot of people own their Games?

      • Solution: Filter the data to eliminate games with fewer than 100 recorded owners.

    • There were many games attributed to a designer named "Jr." After some head-scratching, I realized that any commas in the designer or publisher name created separate entries within the CSV, resulting in many orphaned suffixes skewing the data.

      • Solution: Remap the orphaned suffixes to their original location.
        (Jr., Sr., II, III, IV, PhD, Inc., LLC, Ltd.)


    Now that we've done some cleanup let's see the results.

Findings Analysis


    These are not the results I expected at all. Time after time, the names that top the Best Board Game Designers lists include Rosenberg, Knizia, Cathala, Lacerda, and Garfield. These are the designers that have been around for years. These are the people who have designed dozens, if not hundreds, of well-known games! So what went wrong?

    It turns out that having too many games to your credit will pull your averages down. As the designer scatter plot shows, nearly every designer with 60 or more games to their credit has an average rating below 7.0. Some brief research of their game credits shows a wide swath of ratings- some great, some not so great.

    Sharing the results with my group of board game friends, they were similarly surprised, fully expecting the "Great Ones" to be at the top. The group researched the results, mostly looking for some new games to try. However, after some conversation, one interesting trend appeared. It seemed the majority of the Designers and Publishers in the Top 10 had highly ranked games from the past 5-10 years. Was this a side effect of the board game industry's growth over the last decade? Or, perhaps more board game players are aware of websites like BoardGameGeek, and the volume of ratings for more recent games is higher. I ran a regression analysis on the games from the last 50 years, and, sure enough, there is a 1 to 1.5 rating increase over that period.

    If I were more skilled at statistical analysis, I would like to explore many other facets of this data. Overall, this data worked well for this project, though I would have liked to explore other visualization options as well. Hopefully, this information provides a bit of insight into the world of board games. For my board game group, we'll definitely have some new ideas of board games to bring to the table.
